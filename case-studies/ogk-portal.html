01 — Context

In 2019, Austria merged nine regional health insurance funds into a single entity — the ÖGK. The technology, however, wasn't merged. Five years on, citizens were still navigating a patchwork of legacy portals, inconsistent data, and 9 different user accounts for a single insurance relationship.

The patient-facing digital estate was, in practice, nine separate systems dressed with a shared header. Insured citizens who had moved regions, changed employment, or had dependants on different former funds were forced to maintain multiple logins. Claims submitted through the portal sometimes landed on the wrong fund's processing queue. Reimbursement status was invisible — users could submit a claim and receive no feedback for weeks.

The call centre received 1.2 million inbound contacts per year. Analysis revealed that 64% of these were information requests resolvable by a self-service portal — if the portal existed, was findable, and actually worked. The opportunity cost was significant: each call cost the ÖGK an average of €8.40 in staff time.

We were brought in to lead the UX strategy and design for the unified portal — working alongside three system integrators, the ÖGK's own IT and legal teams, and the Austrian Data Protection Authority (DSB) on DSGVO compliance design.

The institutional complexity: Healthcare data is among the most sensitive personal data in DSGVO terms. Every UX decision — what data to display on the dashboard, whether to auto-populate forms, how to handle family account access — required legal review and DSB consultation. Good UX and regulatory compliance had to be designed in parallel, not in sequence.

02 — Discovery

An 8-week discovery sprint revealed a portrait of institutional failure that had been invisible to the organisation — documented by the people experiencing it.

We recruited a 60-person research panel stratified by age, region, employment status, and disability status — reflecting the actual insured population, not a convenience sample. The research combined diary studies (capturing real interactions over 3 weeks), moderated testing of the existing portal, and a 400-person survey on digital health service expectations.

Weeks 1–2
Stakeholder Alignment & Scoping
18 interviews across ÖGK departments, IT integrators, legal, DSB liaison, and patient advocacy groups (including disability and elderly care organisations). Revealed 34 competing requirements and 6 hard legal constraints on data display.
Weeks 3–5
Diary Studies & Contextual Research
60 participants kept interaction diaries across 3 weeks — logging every touchpoint with health insurance (portal, phone, physical office, doctor interaction). Generated 1,200+ experience moments, coded into 14 recurring pain themes.
Weeks 6–7
Accessibility Audit & Heuristic Review
Full WCAG 2.1 audit across all existing portal surfaces. Screen reader testing with JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, and TalkBack. 214 violations identified — 42 critical. Key failures: no keyboard navigation on claim submission, missing alt text on benefit documents, colour-only status indicators.
Week 8
Co-Design Workshops
3 workshops in Vienna, Linz, and Graz. Mixed participants: insured citizens, patient advocates, ÖGK caseworkers. Prioritised 18 "must-have" self-service features. Created first paper prototypes of the unified dashboard concept.

Top Four Research Findings

1
Claim status invisibility was the #1 source of call centre volume
64% of sampled calls were "where is my reimbursement?" enquiries. The existing portal showed a confirmation number at submission — but no subsequent status updates. Users called because the portal gave them no alternative.
2
Family access management was completely broken
47% of users had dependants (children, partners) they managed health insurance for. The portal had no family account concept — users had to call or visit an office to manage family-related claims. This accounted for 28% of unnecessary office visits.
3
Document upload was the biggest abandonment point
The existing claim submission required users to upload scanned documents. 54% of users without a scanner abandoned mid-flow. Of those who continued, 38% uploaded files in rejected formats (wrong size, unsupported file type, unreadable quality).
4
Elderly users (60+) accounted for 52% of usage — but the design skewed young
The insured population skews older. The existing portal had 13px body text, colour-only status indicators, and jargon-heavy error messages. Testing with 60+ users showed a 23% task completion rate without assistance — far below WCAG's spirit.

Screen 02

Progressive Claim Submission — No Scanner Required

The biggest submission drop-off point was the document upload step — 54% of users without a scanner abandoned. We redesigned this entirely: a smartphone camera capture option (QR code to mobile), email-in option, and graceful degradation to postal fallback — all presented as equally valid choices without hierarchy shaming.

Impact
Claim submission completion rate: 31% → 79%. "No scanner" abandonment eliminated. Mobile camera upload accounts for 47% of all document submissions in the first 6 months.
Screen 03

Family Account Management — designed with privacy and legal review from day one

The DSGVO presented a genuine design challenge: how to let a parent manage their child's health insurance claims without creating an interface that felt like surveillance. We worked directly with the DSB on an "explicit consent + purpose limitation" model — each family member explicitly grants access, can revoke it, and can see exactly what the account holder can view about them.

This approach became a reference case in the ÖGK's internal data ethics guidelines — an example of privacy-by-design embedded into the UX rather than bolted on through a consent popup.

03 — Accessibility

Achieving WCAG 2.1 AAA on critical journeys wasn't a spec requirement — it was the right thing to do for an organisation serving 8 million citizens including disabled and elderly populations.

Healthcare services are disproportionately used by people with disabilities, chronic conditions, and age-related impairments. We argued — successfully — that the claim submission flow, the reimbursement status view, and the doctor search should meet AAA criteria, not just the AA minimum the procurement spec required.

Contrast & Colour
All text meets 7:1 contrast ratio (AAA). Status indicators use colour + icon + text — never colour alone. High-contrast mode available as user preference.
Keyboard Navigation
Full keyboard operability, logical tab order, visible focus indicators at 3px offset, no keyboard traps. Every interactive element reachable and operable without a mouse.
Screen Reader
Full testing with JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, TalkBack. Semantic HTML5, ARIA landmarks, dynamic content announcements, form error association — all verified by blind users in testing.
Language & Cognition
Plain German (Leichte Sprache for critical instructions). Medical jargon explained inline. Error messages describe what went wrong and how to fix it — not just "invalid input."
