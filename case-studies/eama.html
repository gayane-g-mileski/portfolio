01 — Challenge

A portal that had grown organically over 12 years had become a patchwork of contradictions — technically functional but experientially broken.

EAMA.at is the Austrian gateway to European employment mobility services — a platform connecting job seekers with cross-border opportunities, employers posting roles across EU member states, and government caseworkers managing placements. By 2021, the portal had been through five separate development phases, each adding features without a coherent design strategy.

The results were predictable: a navigation structure that required an average of 7.4 clicks to reach key tasks, no consistent visual language, accessibility failures across 34% of tested user journeys, and a mobile experience that had been described by caseworkers as "effectively unusable." Call centre volume had grown 22% year-on-year despite declining user numbers.

The organisation knew they needed a transformation — but they also needed to maintain service continuity for 400,000 active users, navigate a complex procurement and approval cycle, and satisfy legal requirements across three Austrian ministries simultaneously.

02 — Research

Twelve weeks of intensive discovery, involving 84 participants across three distinct user populations.

We ran a phased research programme designed to surface both attitudinal and behavioural insights. The three user types — job seekers, employers, and government caseworkers — had fundamentally different mental models and success criteria, which existing portal architecture had failed to distinguish.

Stakeholder Interviews
24 sessions with ministry officials, caseworkers, and platform administrators. Surfaced legislative constraints and hidden operational workflows.
Contextual Inquiry
38 in-context observations at AMS offices, job centres, and employer HR departments. Captured real task workflows without survey bias.
Accessibility Audit
Full WCAG 2.1 heuristic review plus screen reader testing with NVDA, JAWS, and VoiceOver. 146 violations catalogued and severity-rated.
Co-Design Workshops
4 full-day workshops (Vienna, Graz, Linz, Salzburg) with 22 participants across user types. Generated 340+ requirements, prioritised by impact/feasibility.
Analytics Deep-Dive
18-month Matomo data review. Identified 12 high-exit pages, 8 broken task flows, and counter-intuitive navigation patterns invisible to the organisation.

Key Research Findings

1
Three distinct user mental models — one navigation
Job seekers, employers, and caseworkers had completely different task priorities and entry-point expectations. The single navigation structure served none of them well. 71% of job seekers couldn't locate the "cross-border job search" function unaided.
2
Registration friction was eliminating qualified users
34% of users abandoned registration before completion. The form required 22 mandatory fields — 16 of which our research showed were never used by caseworkers downstream. Legal review confirmed 12 were not legally required.
3
Mobile usage was 58% — but the mobile experience was an afterthought
Analytics showed majority mobile traffic, but the portal was designed desktop-first with no responsive breakpoints on 14 core task pages. Turkish and Serbian-speaking users showed the highest mobile dependency.
4
146 WCAG violations — 28 critical, 47 serious
The most critical issues: missing form labels, keyboard traps in the job search filter, inadequate colour contrast (6 pages failed 3:1 minimum), and no skip navigation. Users with screen readers could not complete any primary task flow independently.
03 — Process Design

Redesigning the architecture before touching a single pixel of interface.

With 340+ requirements and three distinct user populations, we needed a structural solution before any visual design work began. I facilitated a three-day service blueprinting workshop with the client's product team, resulting in a new information architecture that introduced role-based entry points while maintaining a unified back-end service layer.
